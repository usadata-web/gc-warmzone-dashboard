Strategic Context — U.S. Critical Minerals Priority
📌 Headline finding (April 2026): USGS published a quantitative resource assessment in Natural Resources Research finding 2.3 million metric tons of undiscovered, economically recoverable lithium oxide in Appalachian pegmatites — 1.43 Mt in the southern Appalachians (Carolinas, extending Maryland to Alabama; anchored on the historic Kings Mountain belt) and 0.9 Mt in the northern Appalachians (Maine, New Hampshire). USGS Director Mamula characterized this as enough to "replace 328 years of U.S. imports at last year's level," equivalent to ~130M EVs or 1.6M grid-scale batteries. Pulls forward 10-20 years of upstream services activity. H (SRC-186, SRC-190)

Why this is a U.S. national priority

  • China dependence is the core driver. China controls most global processing of cobalt, lithium, rare earths, gallium, germanium, graphite, and antimony. China imposed export controls on gallium/germanium (Aug 2023), graphite (Oct 2023), antimony (Aug 2024), then in Dec 2024 banned exports of gallium/germanium/antimony/superhard materials specifically to the U.S. (SRC-185)
  • Bipartisan policy continuity. Both Biden (EO 14017, 2021) and Trump (EO 14241, March 2025) treat critical minerals as a national-security supply-chain emergency. Trump's EO additionally invoked DPA Sections 301/302/303 and delegated those authorities to DFC's CEO. (SRC-183, SRC-184)
  • The U.S. is structurally short. Lithium imports >50% of consumption with one domestic producer; gallium/germanium/antimony exports from China near zero in 2025, with prices up 365-437%. (SRC-185)
  • USGS 2025 list expanded to 60 minerals (up from 50 in 2022) — added boron, copper, lead, metallurgical coal, phosphate, potash, rhenium, silicon, silver, uranium. Highest-risk commodities: rhodium, gallium, germanium, tungsten, niobium, magnesium, potash, multiple REEs. (SRC-180)

  • Standalone subsidiary model (baseline): We preserve the acquired company's brand, culture, management team, and operating independence. Target's employees, customers, and vendor relationships continue unchanged post-close. This is not a roll-up / consolidation play.
  • Small Business status preservation: Under the standalone subsidiary structure, acquired companies typically retain their SBA size status, preserving access to set-aside contract pipelines (40–73% of Federal opportunities in our target zones).
  • Immediately accretive Federal access: GC holds 8 active Federal contract vehicles (GSA MAS, OASIS+ unrestricted and 8(a), STARS III, SeaPort-NxG, MDA SHIELD, DIA SITE III). Targets gain access to a Federal channel they likely could not easily build on their own.
  • Capital to grow, not strip-mine: GC acquires to deploy additional capital into growth — new geographies, new service lines, new sales capacity. We do not strip costs, offshore, or force synergies.
  • Experienced M&A team + cash: Internal corp dev function. Cash-funded deals (no financing contingency). Proven ability to execute within 90–120 days from LOI. References available.
  • Management continuity expected: We want existing leadership to stay. Earnouts, rollover equity, and retention agreements structured to align long-term. Founder/CEO liquidity paired with active role post-close.
  • Durable, non-extractive buyer: GC is not PE; no fund lifecycle pressure, no flip timeline, no dividend recap. Long-term operator mindset.

Frequently Asked Questions

Q: What if the target is currently 60% Federal? Is that a disqualifier?

A: Generally yes. GC's primary acquisition objective is commercial diversification — if a target is already Federal-heavy, it doesn't meaningfully reduce GC's Federal concentration. Exception: if the target has exceptional commercial growth trajectory (e.g., commercial growing 30%+ YoY while Federal is flat), and clear strategy to continue commercial expansion, it may be considered. Guideline: prefer ≤40% Federal revenue at close; ideal is <30%.

Q: Does the target need SBA size certification?

A: Not required but preferred where relevant. Under the standalone subsidiary model, a target's existing SB status typically transfers and is preserved, giving GC's portfolio a small business channel. If the target holds specific certifications (8(a), WOSB, SDVOSB, HUBZone), flag these prominently — they carry premium value.

Q: What about key employee retention? Rollover equity for founders?

A: Both are standard GC practice. Retention agreements for founders/CEOs and key leadership (2–4 year terms). Rollover equity typically 10–30% for founders who want to stay engaged. Earnouts available to bridge valuation gaps. GC's baseline expectation is that existing leadership continues post-close — we want the team that built the business to keep running it.

Q: Can you close in 90 days? What if the target needs to move faster?

A: 90–120 days is standard for a clean target. Expedited process possible for straightforward cases with organized data room. Cash funding means no financing delays. GC has internal M&A team + outside counsel on standby — we're not building a team per-deal. Board has standing M&A committee that can approve expedited.

Q: Do you have a preferred deal structure (asset vs. stock)?

A: Stock purchase is preferred for most services businesses — simpler to preserve contracts, customer relationships, employee agreements, and any specific certifications (SB status, security clearances, industry credentials). Asset purchase considered for cases where there are material legacy liabilities to leave behind. We're flexible based on deal-specific circumstances.

Q: What's your typical valuation range?

A: 1.0x–1.5x revenue or 5–8x trailing EBITDA typical for services targets. Premium (up to 2x revenue) for: strong commercial MRR, diversified customer base, high retention, growth >15% YoY, differentiated IP. Discount for: Federal concentration, customer concentration, declining revenue, thin margins, no recurring revenue. Each deal priced on specific merit — not a fixed multiple.

Q: How do you handle customer and vendor relationships post-close?

A: Standalone subsidiary model means customer and vendor relationships are preserved. Acquired company continues operating under its existing brand, contracts, and agreements. No forced rebranding. No customer consolidation. No vendor rationalization without target leadership buy-in. GC adds resources (capital, process, channel access) rather than stripping.

Q: What geographies are truly preferred vs. open?

A: Technically anywhere in the USA. Preference for Federal hubs (VA/MD/DC, TX, FL, CA, CO, NC, AL) as integration is easier with GC's existing footprint. But a strong commercial business in a non-Fed-hub state (e.g., Illinois, Minnesota, Washington state) would be seriously considered — we're not going to pass on quality for geography.

Q: Do you do platform acquisitions with a plan for subsequent add-ons?

A: Not in the traditional PE sense (no fund timeline). Methodology

The analysis uses parallel Federal and Commercial pipelines that converge in the dashboard. Each side has its own data sources, classification methodology, and confidence rules. The Commercial side additionally applies a 3-layer forward-view framework (Trailing / Current Momentum / Forward View) to extend authoritative historical data toward near-future expectations.

Federal Methodology — USAspending + Fabric Pipeline

Source: USAspending full database via Microsoft Fabric (Delta Lake). 89 NAICS codes (75 Core + 14 Strategic-Expansion added 2026-04-29 for Industrial O&M / Critical Minerals / Space), FY2022-2026, AWARD records only (IDV ceiling values excluded). Strategic-Expansion zones flow through as Z9_/Z10_/Z11_ capability_cluster IDs (prefixed with Z to disambiguate from existing Track-B keyword clusters 10_/11_/13_).

Step 1 deduplication: One row per award_id_piid (removes duplicate transaction modifications). This alone removed $68.6B in over-counting.

Addressable definition: Contracts with base_and_all_options_value <$250M — the slice GC can realistically compete for as prime. Contracts ≥$250M are mega-IDIQ / mega-program-level and excluded.

Three-layer classification logic:

  • Layer 1 — NAICS code: Coarse sector assignment (first filter)
  • Layer 2 — PSC code: Structured middle filter (product/service code)
  • Layer 3 — Award description keywords: Precision classification via regex (16 categories, ~30 sub-categories, ~460 stopwords tuned through 5 iterations)

Track A (61 narrow NAICS): Classified by NAICS code alone — the code is specific enough to determine the sector.

Track B (14 broad NAICS): Classified by keyword regex on prime_award_base_transaction_description. 63.3% classified after 5 iterations; 36.7% unclassified (military codenames, empty descriptions, opaque text). All Federal figures are therefore floor estimates.

Data pipeline detail: See research/data-pipeline-and-dashboard-process.md and the PySpark notebook research/fabric-notebook-goldcreek-analysis.py (10 steps, 17 output CSVs).

Equal-Weight Principle

Federal spending data is used for context and validation — to confirm real demand and identify geographic concentrations. It is NOT used to rank sector priority on its own. All 8 GC capability categories start on equal footing in the composite scoring. A $5B sector with exceptional growth and strong commercial opportunity may rank higher than a $50B sector with flat trends.

"Warm Not Hot" Standard

Targets must be in sectors with established, durable demand — not hype cycles, policy spikes, or unscaled emerging tech. Yes: PFAS remediation, IT managed services, facilities maintenance, financial audit. No: AI startups, crypto, drone pure-plays, pandemic-driven public health spending.
